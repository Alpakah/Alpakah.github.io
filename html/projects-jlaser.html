JLaserVideo Batmobile

⇩

JLaserVideo | Los Angeles, CA (2022)

JLaserVideo is an engineer on YouTube, bringing fictional aspects of pop culture to real life. I got to work with him on a video, creating a full-size Batmobile made of legos.

I'd been watching engineers on YouTube, and it's a primary reason why I love engineering today. I had been following JLaser's channel for several months until that point and decided to reach out and try to join the team.

My initial task was to design the Lego transport system: I needed to store a cubic meter of Lego bricks inside of the Batmobile chassis and release/propel said bricks behind the Batmobile at will. This is where I learned that (#1) YouTube ad money is pretty significant and (#2) despite having all the funds you want to make the system of your dreams, the best solution is always the simplest solution. For having a seemingly unlimited budget, I surprised myself by only spending $50 on some door hinges, pulley, and rope to create a series of trap doors that can be released at the tug of a rope from within the cockpit.

I dedicated the remainder of my time to mapping, cutting, and adhering Lego panels to the Batmobile chassis and getting to dip into my artistic side with paint, touchups, and even custom lighting for the JLaserVideo logo plastered on the hood of the car

For the shoot, we drove out to the dry lakebeds of El Mirage, CA, and spent a full evening filming content for the video. It was certainly one of the most incredible experiences to say I could be a part of: not only did I get to see my subsystems work on a full-scale in the context of the full assembly, but also to see the intricate details that go into filming a YouTube Video meant for an audience of millions of viewers. The lighting and the prepared scripts were all thought out and covered many different bases for multiple options for use during editing. Yet, there was so much freedom to insert any new angles or ad-libs to improve the final product.

The final video really made me appreciate the importance of documenting the progress of a project through its entirety -- a lot of incredible footage of the team goofing around made it into the final video as footage demonstrating components I didn't even know needed highlighting.
