BruinBot | UCLA (2021-2022)

X1 Robotics at UCLA is the college's premiere robotics organization. At the beginning of every year, the team creates ideas and pitches them to each other, ultimately voting on the single best idea to build over the next two years with the first year dedicated to R&D and CAD and the second year dedicated to manufacturing and assembly, integration, and testing (AI&T). I entered the program as a Junior during the second year of this cycle, where I manufactured certain components and redesigned others -- In this page I touch on the subsystems I had direct involvement in re-designing and manufacturing.

Drivetrain

BruinBot has a simple but powerful two-wheel tank drivetrain, powered by two individual VEX Robotics CIM Motors with 30:1 gearboxes attached to them (one on the left and one on the right) to ensure the drive operates at a high enough torque to withstand the steep hills of the UCLA campus. The back wheels feature standard wheels and the front wheels are omni wheels to allow BruinBot to turn sharper. Head and Face

BruinBot's head is driven by two stepper motors and serves as a 2 Degree of Freedom (DOF) robotic mechanism: the first motor drives the head's rotation about the midline (vertical "z-axis"), and a second motor sits within the head and drives its extension and flexion (the "nodding" motion). If you look closely, the ears of BruinBot also move even more in relation to the head, which is driven by a 4 bar linkage within the head's frame.

BruinBot's face is powered by an LED Matrix behind "Black LED Acrylic" so that the face panel appears opaque when unlit, but shows LED light to pass through from behind. The facial expressions are controlled by Arduino and cycle over time. The full desired function was to create a user interface on the screen that would then prompt certain expressions to appear, but the project did not reach that stage.

Arm and Payload

My personal favorite function of BruinBot was its vending capabilities. When I entered the project, the vending compartment had already been designed, intended to be laser cut out of acrylic, but with no real means of assembly. I redesigned the payload to use a "lock and key" design that would constrain the assembly while allowing for easy assembly and disassembly. This component also relied on precise tolerances for the interference fit between the lock and keys to keep the components in place as the system vibrates.

BruinBot stores snacks in its belly and dispense them like a vending machine onto a conveyor belt that would feed to the left hand, which would then rotate upwards to hand it to you. The arm only contains 1 DOF and instead features a four parallel 4 bar linkage within it so that the hand stays completely parallel to the ground BruinBot is standing on -- a hand that I designed myself to give BruinBot a bear-like quality without compromising its function. I thoroughly enjoy working on both the technical engineering and artistic design of projects like these.

The End Result

This project is by no means completely done by my own self -- the BruinBot team worked incredibly hard to put this project together from scratch with zero blueprint from prior years to build on like certain other programs. The topics covered on this page are all components I redesigned or assembled, but there are so many other components in this complex project that you can read about in the full documentation below.

Ultimately, due to the program having a very delayed start in manufacturing and assembly due to the Covid Pandemic, the project remains unfinished, just short of being complete. At its current state, BruinBot has a fully functioning head/face and is capable of driving around. Unfortunately, the vending system and actuated arm were not fully operational by the conclusion of the project, but it was incredibly fruitful to see most of the project become realized from CAD space to assembly!
