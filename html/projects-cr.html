Sabertooth | UCLA (2019-2022)

Combat robotic events feature fights between bots similar to the TV series "Battlebots™". Where as the TV series fights at the heavyweight division listed at 250 lbs, the general combat robotics scene across the world has a number of weight classes.

Sabertooth is the name for my Beetleweight (3 lb) Combat Robot, which I built as a part of the Combat Robotics program within the UCLA Student Chapter of the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME). I began building Sabertooth upon joining UCLA as a freshman in the Fall of 2019, and continued to build it through the Winter of 2020. However, in March of 2020, the Covid Pandemic would halt all progress, a mere month before competition was supposed to be held. For most in the program, the pandemic would shelf their creations forever. But I was the only freshman in the program that would still stay in the organization to finally finish my work and see my bot compete nearly three years later.

Since I completed the majority of Sabertooth as a freshman, the bot features my earliest work as an engineer -- in fact, my first work as an engineer.

This project was also my first exposure to so many different skills: manual machining, 3D printing, soldering, and electrical harnessing. I learned how to lathe and mill aluminum to 0.005" accuracy, and also waterjet steel cutouts for the faces of the aluminum weapon. I got to model and (albeit, rather crudely) estimate the size and mass of electrical the electrical wiring and harnessing. I even learned about the properties of thermal expansion, freezing the aluminum rod that served as the axle for the rotating weapon in order to reduce its cross sectional area so that it would fit into bearings, pulleys, and the weapon itself, which would then expand as it warms back up to room temperature, expanding back to its original size and ensuring a interference fit.

Slap&Go | UCLA (2022)

Slap&Go is an Antwieght (1 lb) Combat Robot that I built for a 10 week long class at UCLA. I wanted to have some fun with this project and push the boundaries of the mass constraint with a shell spinner, using the entire chassis as its weapon in an effort to put as much mass behind the weapon as possible. I learned very quickly that 1 lb is not a lot of mass for a combat robot, with the electronics and wiring consuming 0.45 lbs alone.

This project was an incredible exercise in mass reduction studies, as the initial design measured out to be nearly 2 lbs. I was able to reduce enough mass from the system to meet the 1 lb requirement; however, this came at the cost of less material surrounding the bolted joints.

Below is a (rather fun) demonstration of Slap&Go's test run, where I replaced its aluminum weapons with rubber hands... so it can "Slap" then "Go". Sadly its fun design did not transalte to much success in the cage, as it lost in the first round of competition. You can see how the first impact tears the weapon BLDC motor off of its mounting fasteners, as the 3D printed PLA was too weak to support the impact. The video for this is also shown below.
